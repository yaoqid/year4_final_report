\section{Introduction and Literature Review}

\subsection{Introduction}
The generation of terahertz radiation via the excitation of semiconductor surfaces with femtosecond laser pulses is a fundamental technique in modern photonics~\cite{Pettine2023}. This process relies on the acceleration of photo-generated carriers, where the resulting transient current acts as a Hertzian dipole source radiating electromagnetic waves into free space. While the physical mechanisms governing these currents—such as the Photo-Dember effect and surface depletion fields—are well-established in theoretical literature, the complex electrodynamics governing the subsequent propagation of the electric field often remain abstract and difficult to visualise.
\newline
The primary objective of this project is to bridge this gap by developing a dedicated simulation interface using MATLAB App Designer. Unlike standard numerical scripts, this graphical interface provides an interactive environment to model and visualise the generation of THz fields based on the method of Retarded Potentials. The specific motivation is to create a versatile tool that allows users to modify intrinsic semiconductor properties—such as carrier lifetime, diffusion coefficients, and absorption depth—to dynamically simulate the radiation characteristics of different materials. By transforming static theoretical equations into observable temporal waveforms and spatial vector maps, this interface serves as both a pedagogical tool for understanding surface emission physics and a predictive platform for optimising semiconductor materials in terahertz emitter design. Notably, recent experimental studies have demonstrated that THz emission spectroscopy can be used as a rapid, noncontact characterisation method for semiconductor surfaces~\cite{YangTonouchi2022}, underscoring the practical relevance of accurate THz emission models.

\subsection{Photo-Dember Effect}

The model is based on the diffusion-recombination theory: a highly non-equilibrium carrier distribution is generated in the semiconductor by ultra-short laser pulses and then evolves under the driving force of spatial gradients and mobility differences. Existing literature has pointed out that the time derivative of the instantaneous photogenerated current is the main source of terahertz radiation~\cite{Hebling:02}. Recent comprehensive reviews of ultrafast carrier dynamics in terahertz photoconductors have further highlighted the central role of sub-picosecond transport phenomena in governing THz emission efficiency~\cite{Lu2022}. Therefore, the numerical framework of this project is based on this physical principle.

\subsection{Electric Field Theory}
\subsubsection{From Photocurrent to Electromagnetic Radiation}
The fundamental link between ultrafast photocurrents and terahertz emission lies in classical electrodynamics. When a femtosecond laser pulse excites a semiconductor surface, it generates a transient current density $\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}', t)$ through mechanisms such as the Photo-Dember effect and surface depletion field drift. Since this current varies on a sub-picosecond timescale, it radiates electromagnetic energy in the terahertz frequency range~\cite{Yang2023}. This principle was first experimentally verified by Auston et al.~\cite{auston_picosecond_1984}, who demonstrated that the far-field radiation from a transient current source is proportional to the first time derivative of the dipole moment. Recent studies have further shown that similar transient photocurrent mechanisms drive THz emission in semiconductor heterostructures, where ballistic photocurrents across band offsets produce measurable radiation pulses~\cite{Malevich2021}. The theoretical framework employed in this project treats each infinitesimal current element on the semiconductor surface as a Hertzian dipole, and the total radiated field is obtained by superposition, following the approach of Johnston et al.~\cite{SimulationTHz}.

\subsubsection{Retarded Potentials and Jefimenko's Equations}
The electric field at an observation point $\mathbf{r}$ due to a time-varying source distribution is rigorously described by Jefimenko's equations, which are the retarded-time solutions to Maxwell's equations~\cite{griffiths_electrodynamics}. The key physical insight is causality: the field observed at time $t$ depends on the state of the source at the earlier retarded time
\begin{equation}
    t_{\mathrm{ret}} = t - \frac{R}{c},
\end{equation}
where $R = |\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|$ is the distance from the source point $\mathbf{r}'$ to the observation point $\mathbf{r}$, and $c$ is the speed of light.

The full Jefimenko expression for the electric field contains three terms: a Coulomb (electrostatic) term proportional to $1/R^3$, an induction term proportional to $1/R^2$, and a radiation term proportional to $1/R$. In the far-field regime, where the observation distance is much larger than the spatial extent of the source ($R \gg d$), the $1/R^3$ and $1/R^2$ terms become negligible. The dominant radiative contribution is then
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r}, t) \approx \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \iint_{S} \frac{\hat{\mathbf{R}} \times \left[\hat{\mathbf{R}} \times \dot{\mathbf{J}}(\mathbf{r}', t_{\mathrm{ret}})\right]}{R} \, dS',
    \label{eq:farfield}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{\mathbf{R}} = (\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}')/R$ is the unit vector from source to observer, $\dot{\mathbf{J}} = \partial \mathbf{J}/\partial t$ is the time derivative of the current density, and $\mu_0$ is the permeability of free space. The double cross product $\hat{\mathbf{R}} \times [\hat{\mathbf{R}} \times \dot{\mathbf{J}}]$ extracts only the transverse component of the current acceleration, ensuring that only radiation polarised perpendicular to the line of sight propagates to the far field. This formulation has been successfully applied to simulate terahertz generation at semiconductor surfaces by Johnston et al.~\cite{SimulationTHz}, who used a semiclassical Monte Carlo model to compute carrier dynamics and then evaluated the far-field radiation using the retarded potential framework. More recently, Kefayati and Nikoli\'{c}~\cite{Kefayati2024} demonstrated that Jefimenko's equations can be combined with time-dependent density functional theory to reveal the microscopic origins of terahertz radiation in spintronic emitters, further validating the generality of this approach for modelling ultrafast electromagnetic emission.

\subsubsection{Physical Interpretation}
Equation~(\ref{eq:farfield}) encodes several important physical features:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Proportionality to $\dot{\mathbf{J}}$}: The radiated field is driven by the \textit{rate of change} of the current, not the current itself. A steady current does not radiate; only accelerating or decelerating charges produce electromagnetic radiation. This is consistent with the Larmor radiation formula in classical electrodynamics.
    \item \textbf{Retardation}: Each source element contributes to the observed field at a time delayed by $R/c$. For a spatially extended source, different elements contribute at different retarded times, producing temporal broadening and shaping of the observed pulse.
    \item \textbf{Geometric projection}: The double cross product ensures that the observed polarisation depends on the viewing angle. For a radially symmetric current distribution arising from the Photo-Dember diffusion mechanism, the transverse field components on-axis cancel by symmetry, while off-axis observation yields a net radially polarised signal.
    \item \textbf{$1/R$ decay}: The radiation field amplitude decreases as $1/R$, meaning the radiated power (proportional to $|\mathbf{E}|^2$) follows an inverse-square law, as expected for freely propagating waves.
\end{itemize}

In the context of this project, the current density $\mathbf{J}$ is obtained from the carrier transport simulation, which solves the diffusion-recombination equation for photo-generated electrons and holes in the semiconductor. The photocurrent arises from the spatial gradient of the carrier density via $\mathbf{J} = -qD\nabla n$, where $q$ is the elementary charge and $D$ is the diffusion coefficient. The Photo-Dember effect produces a net transient current because electrons diffuse faster than holes due to their higher mobility~\cite{Reklaitis2010}, creating a charge separation that radiates terahertz electromagnetic waves. Recent work by Hale et al.~\cite{Hale2022} has demonstrated that even ultrathin (160~nm) GaAs metasurfaces can generate THz pulses with efficiency comparable to bulk crystals, and Jung et al.~\cite{Jung2024} have further shown that InAs metalens emitters based on Mie resonators can produce focused THz beams with amplitudes up to 20 times greater than plasmonic emitters, highlighting how charge density gradients at nanostructured surfaces serve as effective radiation sources through similar photocurrent mechanisms.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Photo-Dember Numerical Model}
First, a one-dimensional electron diffusion-composition model was constructed. By applying Gaussian excitation in both space and time, the expected carrier behavior was successfully simulated: rapid accumulation at the excitation center, symmetric diffusion over time, and exponential decay. The model output was consistent with the analytical expectations, verifying the stability and physical correctness of the numerical method.\newline
Subsequently, the model was extended to two dimensions to reflect the circular symmetry of the real light spot. The two-dimensional results showed that the carriers diffused radially symmetrically and an electron density peak slightly delayed relative to the excitation peak appeared, which was consistent with the ultrafast carrier dynamics reported in the literature~\cite{Lu2022}. Further, by calculating the gradient of the electron density, the corresponding diffusion current was obtained. The result presented a typical transient current structure that radiated outward radially, which was in line with the characteristics of the Photo-Dember current.\newline
To enhance the physical accuracy of the model, additional holes were introduced and a double carrier coupling equation was constructed to reflect the mechanism of net current formation due to the difference in carrier mobilities between electrons and holes. The double carrier model shows that early transport is still dominated by electrons, while in the later stage, the spatial distribution of electrons and holes becomes more overlapping, resulting in a smoother overall carrier distribution, which is consistent with the existing experimental observations.

\subsection{Electric Field Computation}
The computational model discretises the semiconductor surface into a grid of elementary Hertzian dipoles, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:geometry}. The radiated electric field at a chosen observation point is computed by evaluating the surface integral in Equation~(\ref{eq:farfield}) as a discrete sum over the $N_x \times N_y$ source grid:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r}, t_k) \approx \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \sum_{i=1}^{N_x} \sum_{j=1}^{N_y} \frac{\hat{\mathbf{R}}_{ij} \times \left[\hat{\mathbf{R}}_{ij} \times \dot{\mathbf{J}}(\mathbf{r}'_{ij}, t_{\mathrm{ret},ij})\right]}{R_{ij}} \, \Delta x \, \Delta y.
    \label{eq:discrete}
\end{equation}

Each cell carries a time-varying current density $\dot{\mathbf{J}}$. The radiated electric field at the observation point $\mathbf{r}$ is obtained by summing retarded-time contributions from all source elements, where $R_{ij}$ is the distance and $\hat{\mathbf{R}}_{ij}$ the unit vector from source to observer.
    }

The implementation proceeds through three stages, summarised in the flowchart of Figure~\ref{fig:flowchart}.\newline
\textbf{Stage 1: Temporal derivative.} The time derivative $\dot{\mathbf{J}}$ is computed for all three components at every spatial grid point using a second-order central finite difference:
\begin{equation}
    \dot{J}_x(x_i, y_j, t_k) = \frac{J_x(x_i, y_j, t_{k+1}) - J_x(x_i, y_j, t_{k-1})}{2\Delta t},
\end{equation}
applied identically to $J_y$ and $J_z$. Boundary time steps ($k=1$ and $k=N_t$) are set to zero.\newline
Figure~\ref{fig:dJdt} presents a representative snapshot of the computed $|\dot{\mathbf{J}}|$ distribution across the source grid at the time of peak current acceleration ($t \approx -0.03$~ps), which occurs during the rising edge of the femtosecond laser pulse. The heatmap reveals a ring-shaped magnitude distribution centred on the excitation spot: $|\dot{\mathbf{J}}|$ is largest at intermediate radii where the spatial gradient of the carrier density is steepest, while it vanishes both at the beam centre (where the density profile is flat) and far from the excitation region (where carrier densities are negligible). The overlaid white arrows confirm that $\dot{\mathbf{J}}$ is directed radially outward at all azimuthal angles, consistent with the isotropic nature of the Photo-Dember diffusion mechanism. This spatial distribution is the direct source term that enters the far-field radiation integral: the ring-like structure explains the on-axis null observed in the emitted THz beam, since the symmetric radial contributions cancel by symmetry along the propagation axis.\newline
\textbf{Stage 2: Geometry and retarded time.} For each source cell $(i,j)$, the displacement vector from source to observer is computed as $\mathbf{R}_{ij} = \mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'_{ij}$, yielding the distance $R_{ij} = |\mathbf{R}_{ij}|$ and unit vector $\hat{\mathbf{R}}_{ij}$. The retarded time is then $t_{\mathrm{ret},ij} = t_k - R_{ij}/c$, which is converted to a fractional array index $\tau = (t_{\mathrm{ret}} - t_1)/\Delta t + 1$. Since $\tau$ generally falls between two discrete time steps, linear interpolation is applied:
\begin{equation}
    \dot{\mathbf{J}}_{\mathrm{ret}} = (1 - \alpha)\,\dot{\mathbf{J}}(\mathbf{r}'_{ij}, t_{\lfloor\tau\rfloor}) + \alpha\,\dot{\mathbf{J}}(\mathbf{r}'_{ij}, t_{\lfloor\tau\rfloor+1}),
\end{equation}
where $\alpha = \tau - \lfloor\tau\rfloor$. Source cells whose retarded time falls outside the simulation window contribute zero.\newline
\textbf{Stage 3: Cross product and accumulation.} The double cross product in Equation~(\ref{eq:discrete}) is evaluated efficiently using the BAC-CAB vector identity:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\mathbf{R}} \times [\hat{\mathbf{R}} \times \dot{\mathbf{J}}] = (\hat{\mathbf{R}} \cdot \dot{\mathbf{J}})\,\hat{\mathbf{R}} - \dot{\mathbf{J}},
\end{equation}
which avoids explicitly computing two sequential cross products. Each transverse component is then divided by $R_{ij}$, multiplied by the area element $\Delta x\,\Delta y$, and summed over all source cells using fully vectorised MATLAB array operations.\newline
The overall computational cost scales as $\mathcal{O}(N_x \cdot N_y \cdot N_t)$ per observation point. For the typical parameters used in this project ($N_x = N_y = 100$, $N_t \approx 800$), single-point waveform calculations complete within seconds. To generate transverse field maps at a fixed propagation distance, the computation is repeated over a grid of observation points.

\subsection{MATLAB GUI Interface Design}
In this project, an integrated MATLAB App Designer GUI was designed and implemented to interface with the physics-based terahertz (THz) photocurrent simulation engine. The main goal is to provide a user-friendly, interactive platform that allows the user to configure material, excitation, and geometric parameters, execute the full 2D diffusion-recombination mode, and visualize the resulting carrier dynamics and THz waveforms.\newline
The overall GUI structure uses a modular, panel-based layout. Four functional tabs were implemented, including Mechanism Selector, Numeric Fields, Excitation, and $E(t)$ position. These tabs allow users to select the physical mechanism (built-in field, photo-Dember effect, or both), adjust semiconductor parameters (mobility, lifetime, absorption depth, temperature), configure optical excitation (pulse width, fluence, spot size, incidence angle, pulse shape), and specify the THz observation point in 3D space. A separate 3D axes window is implemented to visualize the rectangular semiconductor and the laser beam geometry, including features like incidence-angle-dependent beam rotation.\newline
Callback functions were developed that gather all GUI inputs, validate them, and package them into a structured parameter set for the simulation core. This includes converting fluence to peak intensity, mapping spot size to Gaussian waist, computing Einstein-relation diffusion coefficients, and applying incidence-angle correlations. The GUI supports additional physics toggles that enable the photo-Dember current and built-in drift current to be selectively activated.\newline
The GUI was integrated with the simulation backend, which returns the carrier density evolution, peak-density spatial distribution, and the THz electric field waveform computed via time-derivative current radiation. These results are visualized in three coordinated plots, including central carrier density vs. time, carrier distribution at the peak time, and $\lvert E \rvert (t)$ waveform at a specific chosen observation point.\newline
Consistent axis labels, physical units $(\mathrm{V/m},\ \mathrm{m^{-3}},\ \mu\mathrm{m})$, dynamic titles, and error-handling alerts were added to ensure robustness and scientific clarity.

\subsection{Electric Field Results}
The time-dependent behaviour of the radiated terahertz electric field was evaluated at a fixed far-field observation point located at $x = -20$ $\mu$m, $y = 30$ $\mu$m, $z = 10$ mm. Figure 2 presents the corresponding temporal traces of the $E_x$ and $E_y$ components. Both components display a distinctly bipolar pulse profile. This bipolar structure of the simulated field is in strong accordance with the theoretical model developed by Auston et al.\cite{auston_picosecond_1984}. In their pioneering study on picosecond photoconductors, Auston and co-workers showed that the far-field radiation produced by a transient current source is proportional to the first time derivative of the dipole moment, $\mathbf{E} \propto \dot{\mathbf{p}}(t)$. The results obtained here reproduce this anticipated behaviour: the leading positive part of the waveform is associated with the rapid rise of the photocurrent, whereas the following negative part is linked to the current decay. This confirms that the numerical implementation of the retarded potential accurately reflects the fundamental connection between carrier acceleration and electromagnetic radiation.

To characterise the polarisation of the emitted beam, the instantaneous transverse vector field distribution in the $xy$-plane was examined at a propagation distance of $z = 5$ mm and a time of $t = 0.2$ ps. As illustrated in Figure 3, the electric field vectors display clear radial symmetry, directed outward from the propagation axis. This radial polarisation pattern is a direct consequence of the isotropic Photo-Dember diffusion currents~\cite{Reklaitis2010,Klatt2010}, and is consistent with the angular emission characteristics measured by Mueckstein et al.~\cite{Mueckstein2015}. Analogous radially polarised THz emission has recently been observed in emerging material systems, where terahertz emission spectroscopy has become a versatile probe of ultrafast symmetry-breaking processes~\cite{Pettine2023,Wu2024}.
